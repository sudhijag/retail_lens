# RetailLens Product Evolution Writeup

## Phase 1: Broad Retail Operations Cockpit

In the first major product phase, RetailLens looked like a wide retail intelligence workbench. The app exposed multiple parallel modules, including `Price Intelligence`, `Visual Scanner`, `SKU Matching`, `Alerts`, and `Catalog Enrichment`. The feature set suggested a team trying to cover the full operational loop around private-label monitoring: compare prices across retailers, scan visual listings, resolve product matches, watch alert conditions, and enrich catalog records. Functionally, this phase emphasized surface area and workflow coverage. It was less about a single opinionated decision path and more about giving a merchandising or ecommerce operator a collection of AI-assisted tools inside one interface.

From a product-vision perspective, this phase positioned RetailLens as a broad AI retail cockpit for a private-label team. The implied audience was an operator who needed help across several retail jobs at once, not just a pricing or finance lead. The business story at this stage was expansive: RetailLens could plausibly become the central console for competitive monitoring, product matching, and catalog operations. The strength of that framing was ambition and breadth. The weakness was that the product thesis was still diffuse. It answered many adjacent problems, but it had not yet fully narrowed to the highest-value decision the customer should trust the product to make.

## Phase 2: Category Strategy Pivot After Target

After the Target call on Wednesday, May 20, 2026, the product began to move away from a toolbox model and toward a category-level strategy product. The interface reorganized around higher-order views like `Product`, `Category`, `Scan`, and `Feed`, and the category-oriented experience became more prominent. The product now spent more screen space on market position, price bands, competitive standing, and simulation-style reasoning rather than simply exposing isolated utilities. Even where the individual UI components were still maturing, the direction was clear: instead of just collecting signals, RetailLens was starting to interpret the market structure around a SKU and place that SKU in context.

This represented a meaningful shift in product vision. The audience moved upward from a general ecommerce operator toward a merchant, category manager, or private-label strategist who thinks in terms of assortment tiers, competitive gaps, and where a product sits in the market. The focus became less “help me execute retail tasks” and more “help me understand the category and position my product correctly.” In business terms, this is where RetailLens started becoming a strategic decision-support product rather than a collection of retail productivity features. The Target influence is especially visible in the move toward category framing and the emerging good/better/best logic, which reframed the product around merchandising strategy rather than just marketplace mechanics.

## Phase 3: Finance and Predictive Decision System After Amazon

After the Amazon call on Thursday, May 21, 2026, the product tightened into its most coherent form: a two-part system split between `Price Intelligence` and `Predictive Analytics`. On the feature side, this was a major simplification and a major upgrade at the same time. The current-state side focused on identifying true competitors, understanding live price activity, comparing against market averages, and visualizing position in the market. The future-state side introduced forecasts, time-based price targets, pricing scenario simulation, trend signals, next-product opportunities, and customer-review-derived insight. Instead of a collection of tabs with loosely related capabilities, the product now presented a clearer operating model: diagnose the market now, then decide what to do next.

The product vision also became materially sharper and more commercially valuable. The intended audience now looked much closer to a pricing lead, finance-minded GM, category owner, or operator responsible for margin and planning decisions, especially in a private-label context. The business promise evolved from “AI tools for retail monitoring” into “an intelligence system for pricing and portfolio decisions.” This phase made the product more forward-looking, more decision-oriented, and more legible to an executive or commercially accountable stakeholder. The Amazon influence shows up in the stronger competitor-based insight model, the clearer current-versus-future split, and the move toward forecasting, revenue implications, and scenario analysis. By this phase, RetailLens was no longer mainly about observing the market; it was about helping a business decide how to price, where pressure is building, and what move to make next.
